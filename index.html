Segment Modes

Platform Mandatory

Someone else's creative practice or cultural knowledge is the content — anyone whose practice or knowledge exists beyond this room and is worth bringing into it. Students facilitate; the collaborator is the authority.

This mode requires you to go out and build a genuine relationship before the session. The pre-session contact is not logistics — it is the work. Platform can be attempted in any studio week.

Examples: A local musician performs and talks about their practice. A community elder shares knowledge about place. A visual artist explains a body of work. A scientist talks about their field. A farmer, a land carer, a youth worker — anyone whose knowledge exists outside this building and is worth amplifying.

Performance Choose two of three

Perform. Enact, dramatise, speculate, get up and do something. The creative risk is yours.

Sketch, satire, spoken word, a song, a monologue, a scripted scene. What you bring to the studio is the content. The question is whether you can hold the room.

Examples: A comedy sketch about student media. A spoken word piece about the area. A short scripted drama. A musical performance. A satirical panel show segment.

Participation Choose two of three

The audience or guests are the content — not as spectators but as participants whose presence, response, or action constitutes the segment.

A game, a situation, a designed encounter, a durational experience. The question isn't just what people are doing — it's what emerges. What difference does the participation make? What couldn't have been predicted in advance?

Examples: A live quiz with genuine stakes. A social experiment with audience members. A participatory game that reveals something about the group. A designed situation that unfolds differently each time.

Discourse Choose two of three

Structured dialogue with stakes. Interview, panel, discussion. Requires something genuinely unresolved — not a chat.

The distinction between discourse and conversation is stakes. If everyone agrees before you start, it's not discourse. If the answer is already known, it's not discourse. Something has to be genuinely in play.

Examples: An interview with a local artist about a contested question in their field. A panel discussion on an unresolved issue in local culture. A structured debate. A conversation where something is actually at stake for the speakers.

Note on Explanation: Explanation is a function within any mode, not a mode of its own. The mode determines how.

Clean audio matters more than picture quality. Plan this before you walk in.

Key Moments & Borrowed Looks

Identify the key moments in your segment that need coverage. For each, find a still from an actual broadcast, show, or footage that demonstrates a specific camera or staging decision you want to borrow — a framing, a cut, a use of space. Not a mood image. A decision.

Take a screenshot, save it somewhere you can get a direct image URL from (Discord, Google Drive shared link, Imgur), then paste it into the URL field. It will load the preview automatically.

In the Notes field, name the decision: "single camera, tight on face, held through the silence after the question" — not "intimate feeling."

For each moment: name it, note the shot, describe the specific decision you want to borrow.
